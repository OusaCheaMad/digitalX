inclusive growth

How might we use #tech4good reduce multidimensional poverty and create pathways for prosperity for all?

Supported by UNDP BPPS | UNDP Chief Digital Office | Government of Japan

‍

Why

‍
For the first time in 20 years, the number of people living in poverty globally is expected to rise due to the socio-economic impacts of the COVID-19 pandemic. Globally, 53.1 percent of the global population, roughly 4.1 billion people, do not have access to even one social protection benefit.  More than 60 per cent of the world’s employed population, that is 2 billion people earn their livelihoods in the informal economy. The emergence of non-standard forms of employment, including through the rise in digital labor platforms, is also pushing the boundaries of the informal economy around the globe. The COVID-19 crisis has amply exposed the vulnerabilities of the informal economy.

‍

The #Inclusive Growth Challenge

‍

UNDP will tap the enormous power of inclusive digital transformation enabling UNDP’s programmatic impact to help 100 million people escape multidimensional poverty and vulnerability. This will include digitalization of social protection programs that are designed for inclusion such as of women and delivered at speed in crisis and early recovery efforts, as well as last mile digital delivery and inclusion of citizens to achieve vaccine equity and inclusive health outcomes.  Digital technology will also be leveraged to increase the productivity and resilience of informal economy workers and enterprises, so they can be informed, react, cope and adapt in times of crisis and recovery.

‍

The Global #Inclusive GrowthChallenge is seeking ‘ready-to-scale’ digital solutions that can be used by UNDP and its partners to reduce poverty and try to answer the broad question: How might we use #tech4good to reduce multidimensional poverty and create pathways for prosperity for all?  For example, we are open to, but not limited to, solutions that:

‍

Types of digital solutions we are looking for:

‍

  • Use geospatial data and AI for mapping poverty and vulnerability.
  • Use mobile technology for rapid surveys and assessments of socioeconomic impacts of disasters and crises.
  • Digital tools that allow the vulnerable to access cash assistance and other services from government in times of crisis.
  • Digital registries for comprehensive, universal national social protection systems.
  • Digital tools that extend social security coverage and bring innovative schemes to the informal sector.
  • Digital tools that enhance access to finance and financial tools, mobile money, digital banking and beyond.
  • Digital information platforms that bring market information and market access to informal and rural workers including in the agricultural sector.
  • Digital platforms for jobs and work, with proper safeguards such as minimum wage, built in.
